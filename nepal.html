A guide to the culture of Nepal

Where the Himalaya meets a hundred living traditions

Nepal is roughly the size of Illinois, yet it holds more than 125 ethnic groups and over 120 languages inside its folds — from the high Sherpa villages under Everest to the Maithili plains of the Tarai. This is a working map of its peoples, faiths, festivals, food, architecture and everyday customs.

01 — Overview

One country, three bands of geography

Nepali culture is inseparable from Nepal's terrain: it rises in three long strips, from the flat Tarai plains bordering India, through the mid-hill valleys where most of the population lives, up to the high Himalaya bordering Tibet. Each band produced its own cuisine, dress, architecture and belief — which is why "Nepali culture" is really a federation of cultures rather than one single tradition.

02 — Peoples & Language

Many peoples, one Devanagari page

Nepali (an Indo-Aryan language) is the official language and the country's lingua franca, but it is one of well over a hundred mother tongues spoken across the country. Ethnicity in Nepal is closely tied to geography and history.

Hill & valley

Newar

The historic inhabitants of the Kathmandu Valley; builders of its pagoda temples and Durbar Squares, and originators of Nepal Bhasa and a distinct cuisine and festival calendar.

Hill

Gurung & Magar

Communities of the mid-hills, historically associated with Gurkha regiments, known for the Rodi singing tradition and Sorathi and Ghatu dances.

Himalaya

Sherpa

High-altitude communities of Solukhumbu practicing Tibetan Buddhism, closely associated with mountaineering on Everest and the Khumbu region.

Hill

Tamang & Rai/Limbu

Tamang communities surround the Kathmandu Valley; Rai and Limbu peoples of the east follow the Kirat Mundhum tradition and have their own scripts and calendars.

Tarai plains

Tharu

Indigenous people of the Tarai lowlands with their own language, stilt-house architecture in places, and the Sakhiya and stick dances.

Tarai plains

Madhesi communities

Maithili, Bhojpuri and Bajjika-speaking communities of the southern plains, closely linked culturally to neighboring parts of India, celebrating Chhath and Holi with particular intensity.

03 — Religion & Festivals

A calendar built for celebration

Most Nepalis practice Hinduism or Buddhism, and the two traditions overlap so closely — shared temples, shared festivals, shared deities — that Nepal is often described as a living laboratory of syncretism. Kirat, Islamic, Christian and other communities add their own observances to the same yearly cycle.

Sep – Oct

Dashain

The longest and most important Hindu festival, marking the goddess Durga's victory over the demon Mahishasura. Families reunite, elders bless the young with tika and jamara, and kites fill the sky.

Oct – Nov

Tihar (Yamapanchak)

The five-day "festival of lights," honoring crows, dogs, cows and finally the bond between brothers and sisters on Bhai Tika; homes are lit with oil lamps and marigold garlands.

March

Holi (Fagu Purnima)

The festival of colour, celebrated with coloured powder and water, marking the arrival of spring.

Aug – Sep

Teej

A festival observed largely by women, marked with fasting, red saris, singing and dancing, dedicated to marital wellbeing and the goddess Parvati.

April – May

Buddha Jayanti

Marks the birth of Siddhartha Gautama in Lumbini, celebrated widely by Buddhist communities with processions and lamp-lighting.

Sep (Kathmandu)

Indra Jatra & Gai Jatra

Newar festivals of the Kathmandu Valley: Indra Jatra honors the god of rain with masked Lakhe dances and the appearance of the Kumari (living goddess); Gai Jatra is a cow procession that has become a day of satire and comedy.

Dec – Jan

Maghe Sankranti & Yomari Punhi

Winter solstice-linked festivals; Newar communities mark Yomari Punhi with steamed rice-flour dumplings filled with molasses and sesame.

Feb (lunar)

Losar

Tibetan and Sherpa New Year, observed with monastery rituals, dancing, and family feasts in the high Himalayan regions.

04 — Food

From dal-bhat to the high passes

Everyday Nepali food centers on rice, lentils and vegetables, but each region and community layers on its own specialties.

Dal Bhat Tarkari

The staple meal eaten across the country: lentil soup, rice, and vegetable curry, often with achar (pickle) and meat on special occasions.

Momo

Steamed or fried dumplings filled with meat or vegetables, of Tibetan origin, now Nepal's most recognisable street food.

Dhindo & Gundruk

A thick buckwheat or millet porridge paired with fermented leafy greens — a hill-country staple, especially in the mid-hills.

Newari feast food

Choila (spiced grilled meat), bara (lentil pancake), and yomari, served as part of elaborate Newar ritual feasts (bhoj).

Sel Roti

A ring-shaped, slightly sweet rice-flour doughnut, made especially for Tihar and Dashain.

Thakali Set

A refined version of dal bhat from the Thakali community of Mustang, known across Nepal for its restaurants.

05 — Art & Architecture

The pagoda's home valley

The multi-tiered pagoda roof — later carried to China and Japan along Buddhist trade routes — is widely credited to Newar and Nepali master builders of the Kathmandu Valley, most famously Araniko in the 13th century.

UNESCO World Heritage sites

  • Kathmandu Durbar Square — former royal palace complex, Hanuman Dhoka
  • Patan Durbar Square — Newar craftsmanship, Krishna Mandir
  • Bhaktapur Durbar Square — the 55-Window Palace, Nyatapola temple
  • Swayambhunath — the "Monkey Temple" stupa overlooking the valley
  • Boudhanath — one of the largest stupas in the world
  • Pashupatinath — a major Hindu temple complex on the Bagmati River
  • Changu Narayan — Nepal's oldest surviving Hindu temple
  • Lumbini — birthplace of Siddhartha Gautama, the Buddha

Visual traditions

  • Thangka painting — Buddhist scroll paintings, often of mandalas and deities
  • Mithila (Madhubani) art — geometric wall and floor paintings from the Tarai, made by women for festivals and weddings
  • Paubha painting — the Newar equivalent of thangka, older in origin
  • Wood and metal carving — intricately carved struts, windows and doors on Newar temples and palaces
  • Lokta paper — handmade paper from the lokta shrub, historically used for manuscripts

06 — Music, Dance & Dress

What is worn, played and danced

Instruments

Sarangi, madal, panche baja

The sarangi (a carved four-stringed fiddle) is traditionally played by the Gandharba caste of wandering minstrels. The madal, a two-headed hand drum, drives most folk and dance music, often alongside the panche baja, a five-instrument ensemble played at weddings and festivals.

Dance

Lakhe, Maruni, Deuda

The Lakhe is a masked demon-guardian dance performed during Indra Jatra. Maruni is a courtly dance of the eastern hills; Deuda is a communal circle dance of the far-western hills, sung responsively through the night.

Men's dress

Daura-Suruwal & Dhaka Topi

The closed-collar Daura-Suruwal, worn with a woven Dhaka-print cap (topi), was Nepal's national dress and is still worn on formal and festival occasions.

Women's dress

Gunyu Cholo & Haku Patasi

The Gunyu Cholo (blouse and wrapped skirt) is a traditional hill dress; Newar women in the Kathmandu Valley wear the black-and-red Haku Patasi. Styles vary widely again among Tharu, Gurung, and Madhesi communities.

07 — Everyday Etiquette

Small customs worth knowing

  • Namaste — greet with palms pressed together at chest height, a slight bow, rather than a handshake.
  • Right hand only — give, receive, and eat with the right hand; the left is considered unclean.
  • Shoes off — remove footwear before entering temples, monasteries, and most homes.
  • Head and feet — avoid touching anyone's head, and avoid pointing your feet at a person, a shrine, or a fire.
  • Clockwise around stupas — walk clockwise around Buddhist stupas and mani walls.
  • Tika & prasad — accept a tika or blessed food offered to you with your right hand as a mark of respect.
